Grammar C1

Cause, Effect & Complex Relations

At C1, you move beyond simple cause-and-effect ("because / so") into more sophisticated territory: consequence, condition, purpose, and the ability to chain multiple causes together.

Why this = C1-level English

B2: "I was tired, so I went to bed."
C1: "Given that I hadn't slept properly in days, I decided to prioritise rest in order to be productive the following morning."

C1 speakers don't just state cause-and-effect — they frame the context, show the mechanism, and indicate intention. This is what separates competent speech from truly fluent speech.

C1 Linking Words

as a result / consequently / therefore formal result essay writing — "The policy was poorly planned. Consequently, it failed."
owing to / due to formal cause — "Owing to the weather, flights were cancelled."
provided that / unless condition — "We'll proceed, provided that everyone agrees."
in order to / so as to purpose — "She moved to Spain in order to study art."
as a result of which / thereby chain effect formal/academic — "Investment increased, as a result of which employment rose."
given that / seeing that context/assumption — "Given that the deadline is tomorrow, we need to act now."
in that case / under those circumstances responding to situation — "If that's true, in that case we should reconsider."
Key Phrases C1

Phrases That Do More Than One Job

provided that / unless condition negotiation

"I'll accept the offer, provided that there's flexible working." — setting a condition

"Unless we hear from you by Friday, we'll assume you're not interested." — warning/signaling urgency

therefore / consequently formal result essay structure

"The data was incomplete. Therefore, no firm conclusions could be drawn." — academic writing

"He had no experience. Consequently, he was rejected." — formal cause-effect

given that / seeing that setting context acknowledging reality

"Given that the economy is struggling, spending more seems unwise." — context-driven argument

"Seeing that you've already finished, perhaps you could help me?" — acknowledging what's already true

thereby / as a result of which chain of effects formal/academic only

"The company cut costs, thereby increasing profits." — direct consequence

"Technology improved, as a result of which productivity soared." — formal cause-effect chain

Speaking Practice C1

Scene 1: Panel Discussion — Climate Policy

You're part of a panel discussing environmental policy. Use cause-effect and condition language to build a sophisticated argument with multiple layers.

• What do you think is the single biggest cause of climate change, and what should be done about it?
I would argue that over-reliance on fossil fuels is the primary driver. Given that energy demand continues to rise, we need to invest in renewables urgently. If we don't act now, the effects will be irreversible. Consequently, governments that fail to transition not only harm the environment but also damage their own economies in the long term. Provided that there is international cooperation, I believe progress is still possible.
• Some people say individual action doesn't matter — only government policy can fix this. What do you think?
I see both sides. Owing to the scale of the problem, it's true that individual actions alone won't suffice. That said, I think systemic change often starts with public pressure. When individuals adopt sustainable habits, as a result politicians face pressure to act. So the two are connected. Unless both happen simultaneously, however, I doubt we'll see meaningful change in time.
Useful linking words for this scene:
Given that...Consequently...Provided that...Owing to...Unless...When...as a result...
Speaking Practice C1

Scene 2: Academic Presentation

You're giving a short presentation on a topic you know well (technology, education, economics). Use formal linking words to structure your argument and show complex cause-and-effect relationships.

• Introduce your topic and explain why it matters.
Today I'd like to discuss the impact of artificial intelligence on employment. Given that AI adoption is accelerating across industries, this question is more urgent than ever. Rather than making people redundant, I would argue that AI is transforming the nature of work. However, this transformation comes with significant challenges, notably the need for workers to adapt their skills.
Useful linking words for this scene:
Given that...I would argue that...However...notablythereby
Speaking Practice C1

Scene 3: A Negotiation

You're negotiating a contract, a salary, or a business deal. Use precise linking language to set conditions, indicate consequences, and explore options professionally.

• You want a higher salary. Explain what would need to change for you to accept the offer.
Thank you for the offer. Before I accept, I'd like to discuss the terms. I'm very interested in the role. However, the salary is below what I expected. Provided that we could agree on a higher figure, I would be happy to accept. Additionally, I'd like to discuss the possibility of flexible working. Under those circumstances, I think we could reach an agreement quite quickly.
Useful linking words for this scene:
However...Provided that...Additionally...Under those circumstances...Unless...
