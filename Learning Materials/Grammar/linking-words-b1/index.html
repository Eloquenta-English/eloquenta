Grammar B1

What Are Linking Words?

Linking words (also called connectives or transition words) are the glue between your ideas. Without them, your speech sounds like a list of separate sentences. With them, you sound fluent and organised.

Why linking words = advanced English

At A2, learners say: "I like coffee. I drink it every morning."
At B1+, you say: "I like coffee, and I tend to drink it every morning because it helps me wake up."

Examiners listen for these in the Coherence & Cohesion marking criterion. Using them well can move you from Band 5 to Band 6.

B1 Linking Words: Addition & Sequence

and addition — I studied hard and passed the exam.
also addition — I like reading. I also enjoy writing.
too / as well addition — She speaks French, and I do too.
first / then / after that / finally sequence — First we had dinner. Then we watched a film.
but / however contrast — I wanted to go, but it was raining.
because / so cause/effect — I was tired so I went to bed early.
Key Phrases B1

Phrases That Do More Than One Job

because cause explaining

"I chose this course because it was practical." — cause/reason

"Because the weather was bad, we stayed inside." — same function, different position

but contrast softening disagreement

"I liked the food, but it was expensive." — direct contrast

"But wouldn't it be better to wait?" — polite disagreement

so result moving to action

"I was hungry, so I made a sandwich." — result

"So, what happened next?" — moving the conversation forward

also adding info what's more

"The job pays well. It's also close to my house." — adding a benefit

Speaking Practice B1

Scene 1: At the Cafe

You and a friend are describing your morning routine. Use linking words to connect these ideas naturally.

• What do you usually have for breakfast? Tell us about your morning, step by step.
For breakfast, I usually have toast and coffee. First, I wake up and make coffee. Then I have a shower, and afterwards I sit down to eat. I don't have much time, so it's always quite quick. I also grab a piece of fruit to take with me.
• Describe a time your routine went wrong. What happened?
Last Monday was a disaster. I woke up late, so I missed the bus. First, I tried to get a taxi. But there were none available. In the end, I had to walk, and I arrived 20 minutes late. My boss was understanding, though.
Useful linking words for this scene:
First...Then...After that...In the end...so...but...though
Speaking Practice B1

Scene 2: Job Interview

You are in an interview for a part-time role at an office. The interviewer asks about your experience. Answer using linking words to sound organised and professional.

• Tell me about a time you worked in a team.
At university, I did a group project. First, we decided who would do what. I chose to do the research, and another person agreed to write the report. However, we disagreed about the structure. In the end, we found a compromise, and we got a good grade.
• Why do you want this job?
I'm interested in this role because it matches my skills in communication. Also, I would like to gain experience in an office environment. What's more, I think this company has a good reputation for training its staff.
Useful linking words for this scene:
First...However...In the end...becauseAlso...What's more...
Speaking Practice B1

Scene 3: Describing a Trip

A friend asks about your last holiday. Describe it using linking words to make the story flow like a real narrative.

• Where did you go? Tell the story from beginning to end.
Last summer I went to Portugal. First, we flew to Lisbon and stayed for three days. Then we took a train to the south coast. The beaches were beautiful, so we spent most of our time swimming. After that, we tried some local food, and it was delicious. Finally, we flew home, feeling relaxed.
Useful linking words for this scene:
First...Then...After that...Finally...so...
Practice & Homework B1

Your Turn

Practice using linking words in your own sentences. Record yourself if you can — hearing your own progress is the best motivation.

Writing task

Write 5 sentences about your daily routine. Use a different linking word in each one: and, but, because, so, then.

Speaking task

Describe your journey to school or work today. Use: First, Then, After that, Finally.

Reading task

Find an article online about a topic you enjoy. Underline every linking word you find. Notice how many the writer uses — probably more than you expect.
