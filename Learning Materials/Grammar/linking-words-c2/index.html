Grammar C2

Hedging, Nuance & Register Control

At C2, the question is no longer "Can I connect my ideas?" It's "Can I control exactly how strongly I say something, and shift seamlessly between formal and informal registers?"

Why this = C2-level English

C1: "Social media causes mental health problems."
C2: "There is compelling evidence to suggest that excessive social media use may be a contributing factor in the rise of certain mental health issues, though it would be an oversimplification to claim a direct causal relationship."

C2 speakers calibrate. They know when to be direct and when to soften. They use hedging not from uncertainty, but from precision. This is what makes someone sound truly educated rather than merely fluent.

C2 Linking & Hedging Devices

tends to / has a tendency to softened claim — "He tends to overcomplicate things." (not "He always...")
it could be argued that / one might contend academic distance — "It could be argued that the policy was counterproductive."
to some extent / in a sense partial agreement — "To some extent, I agree with that analysis."
it is worth noting that / it bears mentioning formal emphasis — "It is worth noting that these results are preliminary."
be that as it may / having said all that elegant pivot — "Be that as it may, we still need to make a decision."
in much the same way / by the same token sophisticated parallel — "Children need boundaries. By the same token, they also need freedom."
for all / for all that literary concession — "For all his intelligence, he lacks common sense."
insofar as / in that precise limitation — "This is true insofar as we're discussing short-term effects."
Key Phrases C2

Phrases That Do More Than One Job

tends to / has a tendency to softening describing patterns

"People tends to resist change when they feel uncertain." — describing a general pattern without absolute claim

"She has a tendency to overthink decisions." — describing someone's character pattern

it could be argued that academic distance presenting others' views

"It could be argued that capitalism has failed the poorest." — presenting a view without fully endorsing it

"It could be argued either way." — showing the issue is genuinely debatable

to some extent / in a sense partial agreement nuanced position

"To some extent, I agree — but there are important exceptions." — agreeing with limits

"In a sense, we're all responsible." — reframing the question

insofar as precise limitation defining scope

"This applies insofar as the data is reliable." — setting a boundary on your claim

"I agree in that the principle is sound, but the execution is flawed." — agreeing on one specific aspect

Speaking Practice C2

Scene 1: Academic Seminar

You're in a graduate-level seminar discussing a complex topic. Use hedging, register shifts, and precise limitation to show you can handle nuance at the highest level.

• "Is artificial intelligence a threat to human employment?" Discuss with appropriate nuance.
It could be argued that AI poses a significant threat to certain types of employment, insofar as routine tasks are concerned. That said, it is worth noting that previous technological revolutions tended to create new roles even as they eliminated old ones. Be that as it may, the current pace of change is unprecedented, and to some extent, I think we are underestimating the social disruption. One might contend that the real issue is not job loss but the quality of the jobs that remain.
• "To what extent can individuals really make a difference on climate change?"
In a sense, the question itself is misleading. For all the emphasis on individual action, the structural causes are systemic. By the same token, dismissing individual choices entirely would be an oversimplification. To some extent, consumer behaviour drives corporate decisions — insofar as companies respond to market pressure. Having said all that, I think the most meaningful individual action is political engagement, not recycling.

Speaking Practice C2

Scene 2: High-Stakes Business Meeting

You're in a senior meeting where the language needs to be diplomatic, precise, and carefully calibrated. One wrong word could damage a relationship. Use hedging and register control to navigate this professionally.

• A colleague presents a plan you think is flawed. Express your concerns diplomatically.
Thank you for that presentation. It is worth noting that there are some aspects I think we should examine more closely. To some extent, the timeline seems optimistic, insofar as we haven't accounted for regulatory approval. One might contend that a phased approach would reduce risk. Be that as it may, I think the overall direction is sound — we just need to adjust the implementation.

Speaking Practice C2

Scene 3: Presenting a Balanced Argument

Give a short presentation on a topic where you need to show multiple perspectives, hedge appropriately, and still arrive at a clear personal conclusion. This is the hallmark of C2-level discourse.

• "Should universities be free for everyone?" Present a balanced argument.
This is a question where both sides have legitimate points. It could be argued that education is a public good, and in that sense, it should be funded collectively. For all the idealism behind free tuition, however, we have to consider sustainability. Countries that offer free university tend to fund it through higher taxation, which is not without its own consequences. By the same token, charging fees creates barriers that reinforce inequality. To some extent, the answer depends on what kind of society we want to be. My own view is that at least the first degree should be free, provided that funding is ring-fenced properly. Having said all that, the quality of education matters more than the price tag.

Practice & Homework C2

Your Turn

Writing task

Write an academic-style essay (300 words) on a complex topic of your choice. Use at least 8 hedging/nuance devices. Focus on calibrating the strength of every claim you make.

Speaking task

Record yourself for 4 minutes discussing a controversial topic. Challenge yourself: for every strong claim you make, add a hedge or limitation. Then record it again being more direct. Compare the two — which sounds more intelligent?

Reading task

Read an academic paper or long-form journalism (The Economist, Aeon, The Atlantic). Highlight every hedging phrase. Notice how rarely top writers make absolute claims. Build a personal "hedging toolkit" from what you find.
